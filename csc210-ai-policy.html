CSC210 & CSC210L

Artificial Intelligence Usage Policy - Computer Organization & Architecture

Quick Navigation:

Overview

This policy outlines the appropriate use of artificial intelligence tools in CSC210 (Computer Organization & Architecture) and CSC210L (Computer Organization Lab). While AI can be a powerful learning aid for understanding digital logic, assembly programming, and CPU design, it must be used responsibly to support genuine learning and maintain academic integrity.

Recommended AI Tool: Course-Specific Tutor (Alex Chen)

Primary Recommendation: Use the CSC210 Course Tutor

Students are strongly encouraged to use the provided CSC210 Course Tutor ("Alex Chen") as their primary AI learning assistant. This tutor has been specifically configured for our course content, teaching methodology, and learning objectives.

Benefits of the Course Tutor:

  • Tailored to CSC210/CSC210L curriculum covering digital logic, MIPS assembly, and CPU design
  • Aligned with "But How Do It Know?" textbook and course progression
  • Designed to guide discovery rather than provide direct solutions
  • Familiar with Logisim circuit design and MARS assembly programming
  • Understands the checkpoint system and progressive CPU building approach
  • Programmed to encourage building everything from basic gates (no pre-built components)

Critical Understanding: Full AI Capabilities

Important Notice

While the CSC210 Course Tutor has been tuned specifically for this course, it is powered by Claude 4 Opus, a fully-featured generative AI system. This means:

  • It can respond to questions on virtually any topic - not just computer architecture
  • It may provide information beyond the scope of this course that could be incorrect or inappropriate for your current learning level
  • It can be led astray if you ask questions unrelated to course material or attempt to use it inappropriately
  • It could potentially provide direct solutions if prompted incorrectly, which would undermine your learning
Stay Focused: Keep your interactions with the tutor focused on CSC210/CSC210L topics: digital logic, number systems, Boolean algebra, MIPS programming, CPU design, and Logisim circuit construction.

AI Bias and Limitations

All AI systems, including our course tutor, have inherent limitations:

  • Bias: AI responses may reflect biases present in training data
  • Hallucination: AI may generate plausible-sounding but incorrect technical information
  • Context limitations: AI may not fully understand your specific circuit design or assembly code
  • Outdated information: AI knowledge has cutoff dates and may not reflect current best practices
  • Overconfidence: AI may present uncertain information about computer architecture with unwarranted confidence
  • Tool-specific gaps: AI may not be aware of specific Logisim quirks or MARS simulator details

Course-Specific Appropriate Uses

Encouraged Uses

Digital Logic & Circuit Design (Weeks 1-4, 9-17):

  • Explaining Boolean algebra concepts and logic gate behavior
  • Helping debug Logisim circuits when wires are red or outputs are unexpected
  • Clarifying the difference between combinational and sequential circuits
  • Understanding memory elements (latches, flip-flops, registers)
  • Conceptual guidance on CPU component design

Assembly Programming (Weeks 5-8):

  • Explaining MIPS instruction formats and addressing modes
  • Helping understand register usage and calling conventions
  • Clarifying control flow concepts (branches, jumps, procedures)
  • Debugging assembly logic (not writing code for you)
  • Understanding memory organization and stack operations

CPU Architecture (Weeks 9-17):

  • Explaining Von Neumann architecture and fetch-decode-execute cycle
  • Understanding datapath connections and control signals
  • Clarifying how different CPU components interact
  • Conceptual help with instruction implementation
  • Understanding performance metrics and modern architecture concepts

Acceptable Uses with Caution:

  • Circuit review: Ask AI to review your Logisim design approach, but understand and implement changes yourself
  • Assembly debugging: Get help understanding what your MIPS code does, but write and modify it yourself
  • Concept reinforcement: Use AI to explain difficult concepts in different ways
  • Checkpoint preparation: Get conceptual help before working on lab checkpoints

Strictly Prohibited Uses

Never Use AI For

Circuit Design:

  • Generating complete Logisim circuit files or implementations
  • Providing step-by-step circuit construction instructions
  • Giving direct gate-level solutions for lab assignments
  • Creating complete ALU, register file, or CPU designs

Assembly Programming:

  • Writing complete MIPS programs for assignments
  • Generating code for sorting algorithms, number games, or other lab exercises
  • Providing direct solutions to programming problems
  • Converting Java/pseudocode to MIPS without your own understanding

Academic Assessments:

  • Any assistance during quizzes, midterm, lab midterm, or final exam
  • Help with checkpoint submissions (your circuits must be your own work)
  • Generating project documentation or technical writing

General Violations:

  • Using AI to avoid engaging with "But How Do It Know?" textbook
  • Asking AI to complete lab deliverables or lecture assignments
  • Using AI to circumvent the progressive learning approach

Course-Specific Guidelines

For Digital Logic Work:

  • Use AI to understand concepts, not to design circuits
  • Ask "Why does this gate behave this way?" not "How do I build an XOR gate?"
  • Debug with AI help: "My circuit shows red wires, what might be wrong?" is acceptable
  • Build from scratch: Never ask AI for pre-built components or circuit files

For MIPS Programming:

  • Use AI to understand instructions, not to write programs
  • Ask about concepts: "How does the stack work?" is good; "Write a sorting program" is not
  • Debug logically: "Why is my loop not terminating?" is acceptable if you show your thinking
  • Trace together: AI can help you understand what your code does, but you must write it

For CPU Construction:

  • Conceptual guidance only: "How do control signals work?" is appropriate
  • No implementation help: Don't ask for specific Logisim construction steps
  • Understanding connections: AI can help you understand how components should interact
  • Build incrementally: Use AI to understand each checkpoint's requirements, not to complete them

Red Flags - When to Stop Using AI

Stop immediately if you find yourself:

  • Asking AI to generate circuit files or code
  • Using AI responses without understanding them
  • Relying on AI instead of reading the textbook
  • Asking for direct answers to lab exercises
  • Using AI to avoid the building-from-scratch approach
  • Copying AI suggestions without comprehension

Academic Integrity & Resources

Academic Integrity Reminders

Remember

  • Every circuit must be built by you from basic gates in Logisim
  • Every line of MIPS code must be written and understood by you
  • Checkpoints must represent your own learning progress
  • Lab deliverables must demonstrate your individual skill development

Documentation Requirements:

  • If AI helps you understand a concept that influences your work, note this in comments
  • Never submit AI-generated content as your own
  • When in doubt about AI assistance, ask your instructor before proceeding

Primary Learning Sources (Use These First):

  1. "But How Do It Know?" textbook - Your primary reference
  2. Course videos and materials - Tailored explanations
  3. Instructor and TA office hours - Human expertise
  4. Lab sessions - Hands-on guided practice

Policy Violations and Consequences

Inappropriate AI use may result in:

  • Zero points on affected assignments or checkpoints
  • Retaking of lab practical exams
  • Course failure for repeated violations
  • Academic integrity charges per university policy

The goal of CSC210/CSC210L is to understand how computers work at the fundamental level. Use AI to deepen that understanding, never to bypass the learning process.
